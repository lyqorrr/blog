Trip to Tanjore

All Logs

A Cultural Getaway - Tanjore, A Place Where Architecture Whispers

Tanjore (Thanjavur) had been on my list for a while. Not for spiritual reasons – I'm very proudly not in the "divine energy" club (I always isolate from them lol). I go to historical places the same way some people go to IKEA: for the structure, the craftsmanship, the angles, and the pure 'humans-built-this?' factor. And Tanjore gave me plenty of that.

But here's the thing – whenever I look at architecture, my brain automatically goes, "Okay, but is this more dramatic than Cologne Cathedral?" It's like having a loud European hype-man living in my head.

Brihadeeswarar Temple

The Brihadeeswarar Temple is a tank of a structure – heavy stone, clean geometry, and a vimana that looks like it was precision-engineered by someone who really liked right angles. Architecturally, it's: solid, stable, symmetric, extremely "I know what I'm doing" energy.

But if we're comparing? Well, Cologne Cathedral basically looks like an architect tried black magic with stone. It's tall, spiky, dramatic – the Regina George of Gothic buildings. Chartres Cathedral? That place literally weaponizes light. Stained glass everywhere, like someone installed RGB lights in the 12th century.

Brihadeeswarar impressed me. But the European cathedrals slapped harder visually. And that's just my personal taste (I like European architecture and gothic architecture more than any style).

Thanjavur Palace

The Thanjavur Palace feels like it was built, modified, rebuilt, extended, and then someone said, "Yeah, good enough." The courtyards are nice, the art is great, and the library is basically "nerd heaven but historical."

But then my brain does the thing again:

Palace of Westminster: "LOOK AT MY SPIRES AND ENDLESS POINTY THINGS." Versailles: "Hi, I put gold on gold because gold alone wasn't enough."

Walking through Thanjavur Palace after visiting Versailles earlier in life is like watching an indie film after a Nolan movie. You appreciate it… but you also remember the explosions.

Architectural vibe comparison:

Versailles: "I AM WEALTH, YOU CAN'T IGNORE ME."
Westminster: "I am democracy but sexy."
Thanjavur Palace: “I am legacy, but watch your head if you value it.”

And honestly? The European palaces hit harder for me. I like loud architecture; it makes my brain go brrrr. Tanjavur Palace is more of a quiet thinker.

Tanjore Art: Precision Over Drama

The Tanjore painting workshops were amazing. Watching gold leaf get placed so carefully made me feel like my hands are unqualified to even hold a pencil. The art is refined, detailed, methodical. Not over-the-top. Not emotionally manipulative. Just pure technique.

Europe, meanwhile, has Baroque paintings where everyone looks like they're fainting dramatically.

Again – different styles, different vibes. My taste leans slightly toward the dramatic chaos, but the craftsmanship here is undeniable.

Food & Everyday Life: Honest & Zero Pretense

Food in Tanjore is like the architecture – straightforward, satisfying, no theatrics. Idlis. Dosas. Coffee. All solid. No fireworks, but consistent comfort.

Versailles probably served grapes on plates worth more than my college fees, so it's an unfair comparison anyway.

Tanjore – A Place That Doesn't Need to Perform

Tanjore doesn't try to be loud. It doesn't try to dominate your senses or compete with European giants. It's architecture that whispers instead of shouts.

And I personally tend to enjoy the shouty ones a bit more. Cologne, Chartres, Westminster, Versailles – those places punch you in the face with their scale.

Tanjore taps you on the shoulder and says, "Hey, look at this carving. Nice, right?"

Both experiences are valid. Both are memorable. But yeah – the European ones left a stronger visual impact on me, purely from an artistic standpoint. And that's just the honest, human, mildly sarcastic truth.
